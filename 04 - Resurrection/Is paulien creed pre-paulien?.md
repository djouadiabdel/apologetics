Is the Pauline Creed Argument a Fallacy?

You are asking: is this circular? Are we using the document to prove the document?

No — and here is why. The argument uses three independent lines of evidence that converge:
Line 1 — Internal Linguistic Evidence

The words parelabon (received) and paredoka (delivered) are not just Paul saying "I heard this somewhere." In Greek — specifically in rabbinic tradition which Paul was trained in — these are technical transmission terms.

Paul was a Pharisee trained under Gamaliel. He knew exactly what these words meant in formal tradition transmission. When a rabbi used qibbel (received) and masar (delivered) in Hebrew — he was signaling: this is a fixed formula I am passing on unchanged, not my own composition.

Paul uses the exact Greek equivalents. This is not interpretation — it is recognizing a technical vocabulary that any trained Jewish scholar of the period would use the same way.

This is internal evidence — but it is linguistic and technical, not circular. It is like reading a legal document and recognizing the phrase "whereas" signals a formal contract clause — you do not need external proof, the vocabulary itself carries the signal.
Line 2 — External Historical Corroboration

Galatians is confirmed by external evidence independently:

The Gallio inscription — found at Delphi in 1905 — is a Roman imperial inscription confirming Gallio was proconsul of Achaia during a specific year. Paul appears before Gallio in Acts 18. This anchors Paul's timeline to a specific historically verified date — 51-52 CE.

Working backward from that anchor using Paul's own stated timeline in Galatians 1-2 — his conversion, his first Jerusalem visit, his second Jerusalem visit — produces a conversion date of approximately 32-36 CE.

This is not circular. The Gallio inscription is a physical Roman monument that has nothing to do with Christian theology. It independently confirms the chronological framework.
Line 3 — The Named Witnesses

The creed names specific people: Peter, the twelve, James, five hundred brothers — most of whom Paul says are still alive at the time of writing.

This is not a claim you make if you are inventing a tradition. You do not write "most of whom are still alive" unless people can actually go verify it — because if they cannot, your entire credibility collapses immediately.

Paul is writing to the Corinthian church — a real community, with real members, some of whom could travel to Jerusalem and ask Peter directly. He is staking his argument on verifiable living testimony.

This is the opposite of circular reasoning. It is an open falsification invitation — the strongest possible form of honest historical claim.
So Is It a Fallacy?

The argument uses:

    Technical linguistic analysis of vocabulary (internal but not circular)

    An independently verified Roman inscription (external)

    Named living witnesses with an open verification invitation (external)

Three independent lines converging on the same conclusion. That is convergent evidence — the strongest form of historical argument. Not circular.
