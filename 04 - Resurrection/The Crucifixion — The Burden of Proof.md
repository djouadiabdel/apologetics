The Crucifixion — The Burden of Proof
The Islamic Claim

Quran 4:157 — "They did not kill him nor crucify him but it was made to appear so."
The Logical Point First

The Quran was written approximately 600 years after the crucifixion. It asserts — without citing any historical source, eyewitness account, or documentary evidence — that an event universally attested in Roman, Jewish, and Christian sources did not happen.

The burden of proof lies entirely with the person making the extraordinary claim.

The crucifixion of Jesus is one of the best-attested facts of ancient history. To deny it requires explaining away:

Non-Christian sources:

    Tacitus (Roman historian, Annals 15.44, written ~116 AD): "Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus." Tacitus had no motive to invent this — he was hostile to Christianity.

    Josephus (Jewish historian, Antiquities 18.3, written ~93 AD): References Jesus being condemned by Pilate. Josephus was not a Christian — he was a Jewish-Roman historian with no interest in promoting Christian claims.

    The Babylonian Talmud (Sanhedrin 43a): "On the eve of Passover they hanged Yeshu." The Jewish tradition — which had every motive to deny Christian claims — confirms the execution.

The argument from embarrassment:

No first-century propagandist inventing a religion would invent a crucified messiah. Crucifixion was the most shameful death in the Roman world — reserved for slaves and insurrectionists. It was a public declaration of defeat, abandonment by God, and social disgrace. Paul himself calls it "a stumbling block to Jews and foolishness to Gentiles" (1 Corinthians 1:23).

If early Christians were inventing a religion, they would not have invented a crucified founder. The crucifixion is attested precisely because it was too embarrassing to fabricate.

The Quran's assertion therefore:

    Contradicts multiple independent non-Christian sources

    Was written 600 years after the events with no cited evidence

    Contradicts the argument from embarrassment

    Provides no alternative historical account of what actually happened to Jesus

"It was made to appear so" is not a historical argument. It is a theological assertion that requires the same standard of evidence it demands from Christians — and provides none.
