Paul Invented Christianity
The Objection

Jesus taught submission to God (proto-Islam). Paul corrupted this into a religion about Jesus — divine Christ, atonement, resurrection theology — none of which Jesus taught.
The Response

The timeline destroys the objection.

Paul's earliest letters — 1 Thessalonians, Galatians, 1 Corinthians — were written approximately 50-55 AD. Jesus died approximately 30-33 AD. That is a gap of 20 years maximum.

But within 1 Corinthians 15:3-7 — written around 54 AD — Paul quotes what he explicitly says he received as tradition:

"For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received: that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures, and that he appeared to Cephas, then to the twelve."

Scholars date this creedal formula — which Paul says he received, not invented — to within 2-5 years of the crucifixion itself. This is not Paul inventing theology decades later. This is Paul transmitting a creed that already existed within years of the events.

The Jerusalem church held the same theology.

Galatians 1-2 records Paul meeting Peter and James — the brother of Jesus — in Jerusalem. Paul's gospel was compared with theirs and found to be the same. If Paul invented the divine Christ, Peter and James — who knew the physical Jesus — would have rejected it. They didn't.

The Gospels themselves predate or are independent of Paul.

Mark — the earliest Gospel — records Jesus's divine claims, his acceptance of worship, his resurrection. Mark shows no Pauline theological influence. Luke explicitly says he investigated eyewitness accounts. The divine Christ is not a Pauline invention — it is in the primary sources.

The objection requires an impossible conspiracy:

    Paul invented divine Christology

    The Jerusalem church accepted it despite knowing Jesus personally

    The eyewitnesses said nothing to contradict it

    The creedal formula existed within years of the crucifixion

This is not historically coherent. The simplest explanation is that the divine Christ is what the earliest Christians actually believed from the beginning — because it is what Jesus actually claimed.
