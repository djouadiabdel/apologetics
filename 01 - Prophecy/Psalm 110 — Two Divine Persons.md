## The Verse

> "The LORD (*YHWH*) said to my Lord (*Adonai*):
> 'Sit at my right hand until I make your enemies a footstool
> for your feet.'"
> — Psalm 110:1

---

## Why This Verse Is a Theological Bomb

David wrote this Psalm. David is speaking. David says:

- **YHWH** — the covenant name of God, the divine Tetragrammaton
- said to —
- **my Lord** — David's own master, someone David personally submits to

Here is the problem: **David had no human master.**

David was the King of Israel. He submitted to no man. In the entire
Hebrew Bible, David calls no human being his lord and master. So who
is David calling "my Lord" in Psalm 110:1?

It cannot be a human king — David was the king.
It cannot be a future human descendant — you do not call your
great-great-grandson "my Lord."
It must be a divine figure — distinct from YHWH, yet receiving
YHWH's invitation to sit at his right hand. [web:830][web:837]

---

## The Hebrew — Two Different Words for "Lord"

This is where the argument becomes precise. [web:825][web:835]

| Term | Hebrew | Pronunciation | Usage |
|---|---|---|---|
| First "LORD" | יהוה | YHWH | God's personal covenant name — always divine |
| Second "Lord" | אֲדֹנִי | *Adoni* | "My lord" — used for humans AND for divine figures |

The key question: does *Adoni* here refer to a human or divine figure?

**The context determines it:**
- YHWH invites this figure to *sit at his right hand* — the position
  of supreme authority and co-regency with God
- YHWH promises to *make his enemies his footstool* — global dominion
- Verse 3: his people offer themselves *freely on the day of his power*
- Verse 4: he is *a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek* —
  a divine-human priestly figure who predates the Levitical order
- Verses 5-6: he *judges nations, shatters kings, executes judgment
  across the whole earth*

No human king in history has done this. This is not a human figure
receiving limited royal authority. This is a figure seated at God's
right hand with universal dominion. [web:826][web:830]

## Jesus Uses This Verse at His Trial

This is the most important use of Psalm 110 in the entire New
Testament — and it happens in a hostile interrogation. [web:836][web:839]

**Matthew 22:41-46** — Jesus is in the Temple. The Pharisees are
trying to trap him. He turns the tables:

> "What do you think about the Christ? Whose son is he?"
> They said: "The Son of David."
> Jesus replied: "How is it then that David, speaking by the Spirit,
> calls him 'Lord'? For he says, 'The LORD said to my Lord: sit at my
> right hand until I put your enemies under your feet.' If then David
> calls him 'Lord' — how is he his son?"
> **No one could answer him a word.**

Jesus is doing three things simultaneously:
1. Confirming the Messiah is David's descendant (human)
2. Proving the Messiah is also David's Lord (divine)
3. Implying he himself is the one David was addressing

The Pharisees — the best-trained minds in Jewish biblical scholarship
— could not answer. Not because they were stupid. Because the
argument is airtight. [web:836][web:830]

---

## The Logic of the Unanswerable Question

Jesus's argument runs like this:

P1. The Messiah is the Son of David (everyone agrees — it's the title)
P2. David calls the Messiah "my Lord" in Psalm 110:1
P3. David called no human being "my Lord" — he was the king
P4. Therefore the Messiah must be more than human — David's Lord
C. The Messiah is simultaneously David's son (human) AND David's Lord (divine)


This is not a trick question. It is a genuine theological problem
embedded in the Hebrew Bible itself — written by David 1,000 years
before Jesus was born. The only coherent solution is a figure who
is both fully human (descended from David) and fully divine (David's
Lord). That is the Incarnation. [web:830][web:831]

## Peter Uses It at Pentecost

50 days after the crucifixion — in Jerusalem — in front of the same
crowd — Peter quotes Psalm 110:1 directly: [web:829][web:833]

> "David did not ascend into heaven, yet he said: 'The Lord said to
> my Lord, sit at my right hand until I make your enemies a footstool.'
> Therefore let all Israel know with certainty that God has made this
> Jesus — whom you crucified — both Lord and Christ."
> — Acts 2:34-36

Peter's argument:
- David did not ascend to heaven → David is not the "my Lord" of Ps 110
- Jesus rose and ascended → Jesus is the "my Lord" David was addressing
- The crowd that crucified him is now being told: you crucified
  David's Lord — YHWH's co-regent — not a blasphemer

3,000 people converted that day. In Jerusalem. 50 days after the
crucifixion. In front of hostile witnesses. With the empty tomb
300 meters away. [web:829]

## Psalm 110 Is the Most Quoted OT Verse in the NT

Psalm 110:1 is quoted or alluded to **at least 24 times** in the
New Testament — more than any other Old Testament verse. [web:833]

Every major NT author uses it:
- Matthew, Mark, Luke — Jesus quoting it at trial
- Acts 2 — Peter at Pentecost
- Romans 8:34 — Christ at God's right hand interceding
- 1 Corinthians 15:25 — "he must reign until all enemies under feet"
- Ephesians 1:20 — resurrection and seating at right hand
- Hebrews 1:3, 1:13, 5:6, 7:17, 10:12-13 — Melchizedek priesthood
- Revelation 3:21 — the throne shared with the Father

This is not a single proof-text cherry-picked by one author. It is the
**theological backbone** of the entire New Testament Christology —
rooted in a Psalm David wrote 1,000 years before Jesus was born.

## The Melchizedek Connection — Verse 4

Psalm 110:4 adds a second bombshell:

> "The LORD has sworn and will not change his mind:
> 'You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek.'"

Melchizedek appears in Genesis 14 — he is the priest-king of Salem
(Jerusalem) who blesses Abraham. He has no genealogy recorded —
no father, no mother, no beginning of days or end of life mentioned.
He receives tithes from Abraham — meaning he is greater than Abraham.

The Messiah of Psalm 110 is declared a **priest forever** in the
order of this mysterious, genealogy-free, eternal priest-king.

**Hebrews 7** unpacks this at length: Jesus is the Melchizedek priest.
Not a Levitical priest (that system ended with the Temple destruction).
An eternal priest after a pre-Levitical, pre-Mosaic order that cannot
be abolished because it was never dependent on the Temple system.

This is why the Temple's destruction in 70 AD does not end Christian
worship — the priesthood it depends on predates and supersedes the
Levitical system by 1,000 years. [web:838]

## Jewish Objection Handling

**"Adoni here refers to a human lord — David is addressing a human king."**

Response: David had no human master. He was the king. The entire
premise of Jesus's question in Matthew 22 is that this reading
fails — and the best Pharisaic minds in history could not answer it.
If "adoni" here means a human subordinate lord, tell me which
human lord David was submitting to — and explain why YHWH is inviting
that human to sit at his right hand with universal dominion.

**"This Psalm refers to a future Davidic king — Solomon, or Hezekiah."**

Response: No Davidic king sits at YHWH's right hand. No Davidic king
is a priest forever after Melchizedek's order. No Davidic king
shatters kings across the whole earth. Solomon built the Temple —
he did not judge the nations. The figure in Psalm 110 exceeds every
human king in Jewish history. [web:837]

**"The Masoretic vowel points are late — the original text may have
read differently."**

Response: The Septuagint (3rd century BC, pre-Christian) translates
both terms as *Kurios* (Lord) — confirming the two-lord reading
predates Christianity by centuries. The Septuagint translators,
who were Jewish, saw two divine-level figures in the text. [web:838]

## The Muslim Objection

**"This just proves the Messiah is exalted — not that he is divine."**

Response: A creature seated at YHWH's right hand with universal
dominion, receiving tithes greater than Abraham, exercising eternal
priesthood outside the Torah's system, whom David calls "my Lord" —
this is not a prophet. This is not a created being. No angel in
Jewish scripture occupies YHWH's right hand as co-regent.

The only category this figure fits is the Logos of John 1 — the
divine Word who took human form, fulfilling both "son of David"
(human descent) and "my Lord" (divine identity).

## The Two-Nature Conclusion

Psalm 110:1 alone establishes the core of Christology:

| Requirement | Source | Fulfillment |
|---|---|---|
| Human — Son of David | 2 Samuel 7 | Born in Bethlehem, Davidic line |
| Divine — David's Lord | Psalm 110:1 | Seated at YHWH's right hand |
| Eternal Priest | Psalm 110:4 | Order of Melchizedek |
| Universal Judge | Psalm 110:5-6 | All enemies as footstool |

One figure. Two natures. One Psalm. Written 1,000 years before
the Incarnation. The argument is not: "Look how well Jesus fits."
The argument is: **"No one else has ever come close."**
