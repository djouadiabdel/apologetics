## The Verse

> "And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of
> Jerusalem a spirit of grace and supplication. They will look on **me**,
> on **him** whom they have pierced, and they will mourn for **him** as
> one mourns for an only child, and grieve bitterly for **him** as one
> grieves for a firstborn son."
> — Zechariah 12:10 (NIV)

---

## Why This Verse Is Extraordinary

One pronoun shift destroys every non-incarnational reading of this text.

**YHWH is speaking in the first person throughout Zechariah 12.**
He says: *"I will pour out... they will look on **me**..."*

Then immediately: *"...whom they have pierced... mourn for **him**..."*

The speaker is God. God says they will look on *me*. Then refers to the
pierced one as *him* — a third person individual who will be mourned
as an only child.

Either:
1. God himself is pierced — requiring incarnation to be coherent
2. The grammar is a scribal error — requiring you to dismiss scripture
3. The "me" and "him" are two different entities — introducing a
   divine figure distinct from YHWH who is pierced on behalf of God

All three options point toward Trinitarian/incarnational theology.
No Unitarian reading — Jewish or Muslim — resolves the pronoun shift
coherently. [web:810][web:813][web:815]

## The Hebrew Grammar

The critical phrase in Hebrew:
> **אֵלַי אֵת אֲשֶׁר דָּקָרוּ**
> *elai et asher dakaru*
> "to me — whom they have pierced"

Breaking it down: [web:814]
- **אֵלַי** (*elai*) — "to me / upon me" — first person, God speaking
- **אֵת** (*et*) — direct object marker
- **אֲשֶׁר** (*asher*) — "whom" — relative pronoun
- **דָּקָרוּ** (*dakaru*) — "they have pierced" — third person plural

**The grammar locks the pierced one as the direct object of "me."**
They look *upon me* — *whom they pierced.* The one being looked upon
and the one pierced are the same. God is describing himself as the
one pierced. [web:813][web:810]

**The Aleph-Tav (את) detail:**
Between "upon me" and "whom they pierced" sits the untranslated
*Aleph-Tav* — the first and last letters of the Hebrew alphabet.
The Greek equivalents — Alpha and Omega — are used in Revelation 1:8
for the Father and Revelation 22:13 for the Son. Their presence here
in an untranslated form is a buried divine identifier in the Hebrew
text itself. [web:815]

## The Pronoun Shift — Three Attempts to Avoid It

### Jewish Attempt 1: "Me" means God, "him" is someone else
"God is not the one pierced — they look *to* God *because* they
pierced someone else."

**Problem:** The Hebrew grammar does not support "because." The word
*asher* means "whom" — a relative pronoun making the pierced one the
direct object of looking. They look *on me whom they pierced* — not
*to me because they pierced him.* [web:810][web:812]

### Jewish Attempt 2: The "him" refers to a righteous martyr
Some rabbis historically identified the mourned figure as Messiah ben
Joseph — a dying Messiah figure in Jewish tradition who precedes
Messiah ben David.

**This actually helps the Christian case:**
If Jewish tradition already anticipated a *dying* Messiah figure
before any Christian influence — the concept of a pierced Messiah is
not a Christian invention. It is embedded in Jewish messianic
expectation. The question is only: which pierced Messiah fits the
text? [web:817]

### Jewish Attempt 3: "Pierced" is metaphorical
"Dakaru means emotional piercing — grief, not physical death."

**Problem:** *Dakaru* (דָּקָרוּ) is used elsewhere in the Hebrew Bible
exclusively for **physical** piercing — stabbing, impaling, killing:
- Numbers 25:8 — Phinehas *pierced* both people with a spear
- Judges 9:54 — "pierce me through so a woman cannot kill me"
- 1 Samuel 31:4 — Saul asks his armor-bearer to *pierce* him
- Isaiah 13:15 — "whoever is caught will be *thrust through*"

Every other use is physical and violent. The metaphorical reading
is invented specifically for this verse to avoid the implication.
That is the definition of ad hoc. [web:813]

## New Testament Quotation

John 19:37 — at the crucifixion, after the soldier pierces Jesus's
side with a spear:
> "These things happened so that the scripture would be fulfilled:
> 'They will look on the one they have pierced.'"

John quotes Zechariah 12:10 directly as fulfilled in the crucifixion.
He identifies Jesus — who was looked upon, physically pierced — as
the fulfillment of a verse where **God himself** says "they will look
on me whom they pierced."

John's equation: Jesus = the "me" of Zechariah 12:10 = YHWH pierced
in human flesh.

Revelation 1:7 repeats it as a future event still to be completed:
> "Look, he is coming with the clouds, and every eye will see him,
> even those who pierced him."

## The Incarnational Solution

The only coherent resolution to the pronoun shift:

God — who cannot be physically pierced in pure spirit form — takes
on human flesh. In that form he is physically pierced. Those who
pierced him look upon *me* (God incarnate) — and mourn for *him*
(the human figure they killed, who was also God).

The verse does not merely predict the crucifixion. It predicts
the **reaction** — mourning as for an only child, bitterness as
for a firstborn. This is not the reaction to a prophet's death.
This is the reaction of people who realize they killed their own God.

> "They will mourn for him as one mourns for an **only child.**"

John 3:16 — "God so loved the world that he gave his **only Son.**"
The vocabulary is identical. The mourning of Zechariah 12 is the
recognition of what was done to God's only Son. [web:819]

## The Context: Zechariah 12-13 as a Unit

The surrounding context makes the messianic reading even stronger:

**12:10** — piercing and mourning  
**13:1** — "On that day a fountain will be opened to the house of
David... to cleanse them from sin and impurity."

Immediately after the piercing — a fountain opens for cleansing from
sin. The death of the pierced one opens purification. This is
substitutionary atonement written 500 years before the crucifixion.

**13:6** — "What are these wounds on your body? The answer: 'The
wounds I was given at the house of my friends.'"

A figure with wounds — received from friends, not enemies. Exactly
the profile of Jesus — betrayed by Judas, handed over by the Jewish
leadership, killed by Rome at their request.

## Muslim Objection Handling

**"The Bible is corrupted — this verse was changed by Christians."**

Response: The Dead Sea Scrolls contain the book of Zechariah and
confirm the text is stable. The Septuagint (Greek translation, 3rd
century BC) renders the verse identically. There is no manuscript
evidence of Christian tampering. The verse predates Christianity
by 500 years in every textual tradition we have.

**"God cannot be pierced — you are making God human."**

Response: Correct — God in pure spirit form cannot be pierced.
That is exactly the point. The verse creates a theological problem
that only the incarnation solves. You are not refuting the Christian
reading — you are confirming why it is necessary.

**"This refers to a future mourning in Israel — not Jesus."**

Response: Revelation 1:7 agrees — the full mourning is future.
But John 19:37 confirms the piercing was fulfilled at the
crucifixion. The mourning is still coming. The piercing already
happened. The verse spans both events — crucifixion and return.
Both fulfilled by the same person.
