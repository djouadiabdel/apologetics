## **1. The Ante-Nicene Fathers — His Biggest Silence**

His central historical claim was: the Trinity was invented at the Council of Nicaea (325 CE) under Roman political pressure. Before Nicaea — nobody believed it.

The devastating response: **Ignatius of Antioch wrote Trinitarian language in 107 CE.** Justin Martyr in 150 CE. Irenaeus in 180 CE. Tertullian coined the Latin word *trinitas* around 200 CE — over a century before Nicaea. [dergipark.org](https://dergipark.org.tr/en/download/article-file/2323819)

These men were writing within living memory of the apostles. Some were directly discipled by apostles. They wrote in Greek and Latin with no Nicaean council to pressure them — and they wrote the Trinity.

A scholarly study on Ibn Taymiyya noted directly:

> *"If Ibn Taymiyya had access to the writings of the ante-Nicene fathers — the logical conclusions of his own hermeneutics should have led him somewhere very different."* [dergipark.org](https://dergipark.org.tr/en/download/article-file/2323819)

He was not fully silenced — but he gave no serious engagement with the ante-Nicene evidence. He assumed without proving that pre-Nicene Christianity was Unitarian. It was not. And he never demonstrated that it was. This is his single largest historical failure in the entire book.

## **2. Isaiah 53 — He Could Not Explain It**

Isaiah 53 — written 700 years before Jesus — describes:

- A servant despised and rejected by men
- Who bore the sins of many
- Who was pierced for transgressions
- Led like a lamb to slaughter
- Given a grave with the wicked
- Who after suffering would see the light of life and justify many

The specificity is extraordinary. Every detail maps onto the crucifixion and resurrection of Jesus — including details that would be embarrassing to invent (grave with the wicked, piercing, rejection by his own people).

Ibn Taymiyya's response in *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* was to argue this refers to **the nation of Israel collectively**, not a messianic individual. [l.b5z](https://l.b5z.net/i/u/6103974/f/Answering_those_who_altered_the_religion_of_Jesus_Christ.pdf)

### **Why This Failed Completely**

**First** — the servant in Isaiah 53 is distinguished **from** Israel. Isaiah 53:8 says *"for the transgression of my people was he stricken."* The servant suffers **on behalf of** the people — he is not the same as the people. You cannot be stricken on behalf of yourself.

**Second** — the servant is described as an individual with a specific death, burial, and vindication. Nations do not get buried in graves with the wicked and then individually vindicated.

**Third** — the Jewish Talmud itself (*Sanhedrin 98b*) interprets Isaiah 53 as referring to the Messiah specifically — *"the Messiah — what is his name?... the leper scholar, as it is written: 'Surely he has borne our griefs.'"* Ibn Taymiyya's interpretation contradicts his own tradition's ancient reading.

He never resolved this. He simply declared the collective interpretation correct and moved on. [academia](https://www.academia.edu/105547798/Reopening_the_Muslim_Christian_Dialogue_of_the_13_14TH_Centuries_Critical_Reflections_on_Ibn_Taymiyyahs_Response_to_Christianity_in_Al_Jaw_B_Al_S_Ah_M_LI_Man_Baddala_D_N_Al_Mas_H)

## **3. Psalm 22 — He Barely Touched It**

Psalm 22 — written by David around 1000 BCE — opens with:

> *"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"*

And then describes in detail:
- Hands and feet being pierced (verse 16)
- Garments divided by casting lots (verse 18)
- Bones out of joint (verse 14)
- Being surrounded by those mocking: *"He trusts in the Lord — let Him rescue him"* (verse 8) — the exact words spoken at the crucifixion

The piercing of hands and feet and the casting of lots for garments are details so specific to crucifixion — a method of execution that did not exist when David wrote — that no generic interpretation survives contact with the text.

Ibn Taymiyya's response: minimal. He argued the Psalm was about David's own suffering and did not predict the Messiah's death specifically. [facebook](https://www.facebook.com/groups/1775480886021172/posts/4530788437157056/)

He never explained how David's personal suffering involved pierced hands and feet, garment lots, and the specific taunt that appears word-for-word at the crucifixion. He just asserted it referred to David and left it there.

That is not a refutation. That is a declaration.

## **4. The Paul Problem — He Had No Answer**

The Christian correspondent who prompted *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* was Paul of Antioch — a bishop who wrote a letter to Muslim leaders arguing Christianity's truth from Islamic sources. [etd.ceu](https://www.etd.ceu.edu/2011/grigoryan_sona.pdf)

Ibn Taymiyya's response was the tahrif al-ma'na position — corruption of meaning only. [etd.ceu](https://www.etd.ceu.edu/2011/grigoryan_sona.pdf)

Paul of Antioch's counter — which Ibn Taymiyya never genuinely resolved — was:

> "Show me one place in the Quran where it says 'the Christians misunderstood their intact text.' The Quran does not say this. It either says the scriptures are guidance, or it says people wrote things with their hands and attributed them to God. These are contradictory claims. Which is it?"

Ibn Taymiyya spent pages on this without producing a clean resolution. The tension between Quran 5:46 calling the Gospel *"hudaan wa nur"* and the Islamic claim of distortion was never resolved — because it cannot be resolved without abandoning one of the two claims. ## **6. The Resurrection — He Had Nothing**

This is arguably the most important silence in the entire book.

The Quran denies the crucifixion. Ibn Taymiyya therefore had to deny the resurrection too — because you cannot have a resurrection without a death.

But the historical evidence for the resurrection appearances is not just theological — it is the **earliest dateable material in the entire New Testament.**

Paul's creed in 1 Corinthians 15:3-8 — written around 54 CE, but containing a formula scholars date to **within 3-5 years of the crucifixion itself** — lists named eyewitnesses:

> *"He appeared to Peter, then to the twelve, then to more than five hundred brothers at one time — most of whom are still alive... then to James, then to all the apostles, then last of all to me."*

Paul is writing to a living audience and citing living witnesses by name — inviting verification. This is not legend. This is a legal-grade claim with named sources.

Ibn Taymiyya never engaged this evidence. He simply asserted the crucifixion did not happen — which means the appearances did not happen — which means Paul, Peter, James, and five hundred named witnesses were all either lying or deluded simultaneously.

He never explained how or why. He just needed it to be false — so he declared it false.

## **7. James — The Brother Who Did Not Believe**

This one is historically precise and Ibn Taymiyya had no answer for it because he apparently never engaged it seriously.

James — the biological brother of Jesus — did not believe Jesus was the Messiah during Jesus's lifetime. The Gospels record this explicitly (John 7:5). His brothers thought he was out of his mind (Mark 3:21).

After the crucifixion — James became the leader of the Jerusalem church, died as a martyr for his belief in the resurrection (confirmed by Josephus, *Antiquities* 20.9), and is described by Paul as someone to whom the risen Jesus appeared personally.

The question Ibn Taymiyya never asked or answered:

> **What changed James's mind?**

He went from skeptic to martyr. The only explanation that fits all the evidence is that something happened after the crucifixion that convinced him completely. His death — dying for what he could have recanted to survive — means he was not lying. He genuinely believed he had seen his risen brother.

Ibn Taymiyya's framework has no mechanism to explain James. The substitution theory — someone else was crucified — does not explain why James, who knew his own brother's face, became a martyr for the resurrection.

Silence.

## **8. The Explosion of Christianity — He Never Accounted For It**

Within 20 years of the crucifixion, Christianity had spread from Jerusalem to Rome, Antioch, Corinth, Ephesus, and North Africa — all based on eyewitness testimony of the resurrection.

These were not credulous peasants in isolated villages. Paul preached in Athens to Greek philosophers. The early church included Roman soldiers, Greek intellectuals, Jewish rabbis, and Roman officials.

Ibn Taymiyya's framework requires all of these people to have been deceived about the fundamental event — the resurrection — at the center of everything they were staking their lives on.

He never explained the mechanism of that deception. The substitution theory — someone else died instead of Jesus — would have been immediately falsifiable in Jerusalem where the events happened, where the tomb was known, where witnesses were still alive.

He assumed the deception happened. He never showed how it was possible at that scale, that early, in that environment.

## **9. The Embarrassing Details — He Never Engaged Them**

Historians use a criterion called the **criterion of embarrassment** — details that the authors would have been embarrassed to invent are more likely to be true precisely because no one would fabricate them.

The Gospels are full of these:

- Jesus's first resurrection appearance is to **women** — whose testimony was legally inadmissible in 1st century Jewish courts. No one inventing a resurrection story would choose women as the primary witnesses.
- The disciples are portrayed as **cowards, doubters, and deserters** throughout — Peter denies Jesus three times. Thomas refuses to believe. They all run. No one fabricating a heroic founding narrative portrays its founders this way.
- Jesus is **baptized by John** — implying subordination to John, which was theologically awkward for early Christians.
- Jesus **dies** — the most scandalous possible fate for a claimed Messiah in Jewish theology (Deuteronomy 21:23 — cursed is anyone hung on a tree).

Ibn Taymiyya never engaged the criterion of embarrassment. He assumed the Gospel authors invented stories freely — but never asked why they would invent these specific details that made their own case harder. ## **11. The Quranic Title "Al-Masih" — He Never Explained It**

The Quran calls Jesus **Al-Masih** — the Messiah — eleven times. [facebook](https://www.facebook.com/groups/1775480886021172/posts/4530788437157056/)

Ibn Taymiyya never answered the question that this title immediately raises:

> **What does Messiah mean in the Quran — and why does Jesus uniquely hold it?**

The word Masih comes directly from the Hebrew *Mashiach* — the Anointed One. Every Jew reading or hearing this title in the 7th century knew exactly what it meant — the specific figure promised throughout the Hebrew prophetic tradition who would come to redeem Israel and establish God's kingdom.

The title carries an entire theological freight — Isaiah 53, Psalm 110, Daniel 9, Micah 5:2 — all of it comes bundled with the word Masih.

By using this title — the Quran is either:

**Option A:** Knowingly invoking the full Hebrew messianic tradition — in which case everything those prophecies say about the Messiah applies to Jesus

**Option B:** Using the title while stripping it of all its content — in which case what does the word mean in the Quran? What makes Jesus *the* Masih and not just *a* masih?

Ibn Taymiyya needed Option B — a content-stripped Messiah title — but he never provided a positive definition of what the Quran means by it that does not borrow from the Hebrew tradition he was trying to reject.

He used the title constantly throughout *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* and never once asked what it means that God Himself chose this specific title for Jesus and nobody else.

## **12. The "Word" Becoming Flesh — He Missed the Internal Logic**

John 1:1 — *"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."*

Ibn Taymiyya's response was that John's *Logos* theology was imported from Greek philosophy — specifically from Philo of Alexandria — and was not original to Hebrew monotheism. [etd.ceu](https://www.etd.ceu.edu/2011/grigoryan_sona.pdf)

### **Why This Collapses Completely**

**First** — the Quran itself calls Jesus *Kalimatullah* — the Word of God. If the Word of God theology is a Greek philosophical corruption of pure monotheism — why did God use this exact title for Jesus in the Quran? Ibn Taymiyya gave Jesus the title he was trying to destroy the theological significance of. He never resolved this.

**Second** — the Logos concept is not uniquely Greek. The Hebrew concept of *Dabar YHWH* — the Word of the Lord — appears over 240 times in the Old Testament as an active agent of divine action. It creates (Psalm 33:6 — *"By the word of the Lord the heavens were made"*), heals, and comes to prophets as a distinct communicative presence. The Word in John 1 has deep roots in Hebrew theology — not just Greek philosophy.

**Third** — even if John used Greek philosophical language to express a Hebrew concept — that is called **contextualization**, not corruption. Paul used Greek philosophical terms. The entire New Testament was written in Greek. Language does not corrupt theology.

Ibn Taymiyya's argument proves too much — by his logic, any use of existing cultural vocabulary to express theological truth is corruption, which means the Quran's use of pre-Islamic Arabic vocabulary for theological concepts is equally corrupted.

## **13. Daniel 9 — He Avoided It Entirely**

Daniel 9:24-27 is the most precise chronological prophecy in the entire Old Testament:

> *"Seventy weeks are decreed for your people and your holy city... Know therefore and understand: from the going forth of the command to restore and build Jerusalem until an Anointed One, a Prince, there shall be seven weeks and sixty-two weeks... And after the sixty-two weeks, the Anointed One shall be cut off and shall have nothing."*

The calculation: from the decree of Artaxerxes to rebuild Jerusalem (458 BCE or 444 BCE depending on which decree) — counting 483 years (69 weeks of years) — lands precisely in the range of 26-33 CE. The period of Jesus's ministry and death.

The Anointed One — the Masih — is **cut off** — killed — after exactly 483 years from a historically dateable decree.

Ibn Taymiyya never engaged Daniel 9 seriously in *Al-Jawab al-Sahih*. For a man writing 800 pages on why the Old Testament does not predict Jesus — this is not an oversight. It is avoidance.

Because Daniel 9 is not a vague poem. It is a **mathematical prediction** with a dateable starting point, a specific figure (Masih), a specific event (being cut off / killed), and an outcome that lands precisely in the 1st century CE.

No alternative interpretation that keeps the prophecy within Jewish history has ever successfully accounted for all the elements simultaneously. Ibn Taymiyya knew this. He said almost nothing about it.

## **14. The Disciples' Transformed Lives — He Had No Sociological Answer**

This is not a theological argument. It is a historical and psychological one — and it is one of the strongest.

The disciples of Jesus — after the crucifixion — were hiding behind locked doors in fear for their lives (John 20:19). They had watched their leader executed in the most humiliating way possible.

Within weeks — they were preaching publicly in Jerusalem, the same city where it happened, to the same crowds who had called for the crucifixion. Within years — virtually every one of them died for this testimony.

People die for what they **believe** to be true even when it is false. But people do not die for what they **know** to be false.

If the resurrection was fabricated — the disciples fabricated it. Which means they knowingly invented it — and then died rather than admit they had invented it, even when recanting would have saved their lives.

This is not psychologically coherent. People who invent stories to gain power and influence do not then choose torture and death when the story fails to protect them.

Ibn Taymiyya never engaged this argument. He assumed deception or delusion without explaining the mechanism by which eleven frightened men simultaneously decided to die for something they knew had not happened.

## **15. The Enemy Conversions — Paul and James Again, Deeper**

This goes beyond what I mentioned earlier.

The two most important conversions in early Christianity are:

He had no reason to convert — conversion cost him everything: his status, his career, his relationships, his safety, and eventually his life.

**James** — Jesus's own biological brother who thought Jesus was insane during his ministry.

Both of them claimed the risen Jesus appeared to them personally. Both of them died for this claim.

Ibn Taymiyya's framework requires both of these men to have been deceived or delusional. But:

- Paul's conversion happened suddenly and dramatically with specific physical effects (Acts 9) — corroborated by his own letters written years before Acts was composed
- James knew his brother's face — the substitution theory cannot explain his conversion
- Neither man had anything to gain and everything to lose

Ibn Taymiyya never asked: **what would it take to convert the man who was actively murdering Christians?** And what actually did it?

He just needed Paul to be wrong — so Paul was wrong. No mechanism. No explanation. Just the conclusion.

Christians pointed to Isaiah 53, Psalm 22, Psalm 110, Daniel 9, Proverbs 30:4, Isaiah 9:6, Micah 5:2.

But he never answered the reverse question:

> **"By your own standard — show me where the Old Testament predicts a prophet coming after Jesus who would abrogate his message, be born in Arabia, and establish a new legal system."**

The standard Muslim answer is Deuteronomy 18:18 (*"a prophet like Moses"*) and John 14:16 (*"the Paraclete"*).

But:

- Deuteronomy 18:18 describes a prophet **like Moses** — from among the Israelites, not from Ishmael's line
- John 14:16 — Ibn Taymiyya himself agreed the Gospel text is intact. The intact Greek text says *Parakletos* — Comforter/Helper — which Jesus explicitly says *"the Father will send in my name"* and *"will be with you forever"* and *"you know him, for he dwells with you and will be in you."* None of this describes a human prophet. Ibn Taymiyya spent considerable energy on the Paraclete argument in *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* — arguing the original word was *Periklytos* (praised one = Muhammad) not *Parakletos.* [muslimhope](https://www.muslimhope.com/RebuttalToAlJawabAsSahih3_UniversalityOfMohammedsProphethood.html)

But he admitted the text is intact. The intact Greek manuscripts — thousands of them across multiple centuries — all say *Parakletos.* Not one manuscript says *Periklytos.* He provided zero manuscript evidence for the variant he needed.

He demanded manuscript evidence from Christians for their readings. He provided none for his own most critical claim.

That asymmetry — demanding evidence from others while exempting himself from the same standard — was noticed by his Christian contemporaries and never answered.
