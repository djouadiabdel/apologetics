## The Argument in One Paragraph

The messianic prophecies do not work as a vague checklist that anyone
could fit. They form a *convergent sequence* — a set of conditions that
must be fulfilled simultaneously, by the same person, within a specific
historical window that closed permanently in 70 AD. After 70 AD it
became structurally impossible for any candidate to even *claim* to meet
the requirements — because the genealogical records were destroyed, the
Temple was gone, and the sacrificial system ended. Either the Messiah
came before 70 AD — or the prophecies permanently failed. There is only
one pre-70 AD candidate who fits the sequence. This is not a matter of
interpretation. It is a matter of historical elimination.

---

## The Closed Window — Why This Argument Is Unfalsifiable

This is the foundation. Everything else builds on it.

**Daniel 9:26 states explicitly:**
> "After the sixty-two weeks, the Messiah will be *cut off* — and the
> people of the prince who is to come will destroy the city and the
> sanctuary."

The sequence is locked in:
1. Messiah appears
2. Messiah is *cut off* (killed)
3. **After that** — the city and Temple are destroyed

The Temple was destroyed in **70 AD**. This means the Messiah had to
appear AND be killed **before 70 AD**. This is not Christian
interpretation — it is the plain sequence of Daniel 9:26.

**Why this is unfalsifiable:**
- If the Messiah has not yet come → the prophecy already failed,
  because the destruction happened on schedule and the Messiah was
  supposed to precede it
- If someone claims to be the Messiah today → they cannot verify
  Davidic lineage (records destroyed in 70 AD), cannot restore Temple
  sacrifice (requires verified priesthood + red heifer), and are
  2,000 years outside the prophetic window
- The window is closed. It cannot reopen.

The only logically consistent position is: **the Messiah came before
70 AD — or the Hebrew prophets were wrong.** [web:798][web:805]

## The Daniel 9 Countdown — The Mathematics

Daniel 9:25-26 gives an explicit timeline: [web:796][web:802]

> "From the issuing of the decree to restore and rebuild Jerusalem —
> until Messiah the Prince — there will be **7 weeks and 62 weeks**."

**The calculation:**
- 7 + 62 = **69 weeks** of years (a "week" = 7 years in Hebrew
  prophetic literature — *shavuim*)
- 69 × 7 = **483 years**
- Starting point: Decree of Artaxerxes to rebuild Jerusalem
  — **445 BC** (Nehemiah 2:1-8)
- 483 years later using the prophetic calendar (360-day years):
  lands precisely at **30-33 AD** — the ministry and death of Jesus

**Then Daniel 9:26 continues:**
> "After the sixty-two weeks the Messiah will be **cut off** and will
> have nothing — and the people of the prince who is to come will
> **destroy the city and the sanctuary.**"

The Messiah is killed. Then the city is destroyed. Exact sequence.
Exact timing. Fulfilled in exact order.

**The Jewish counter:** "The calculation is manipulated."  
**Response:** Even granting maximum skepticism about the exact
start date — every proposed start date lands the 483-year window
*somewhere* in the first century AD. No calculation puts it in the
medieval period, no calculation puts it before the Maccabees. The
window is first century regardless of which decree you use.

## The Convergence Argument — Not One Prophecy But a Sequence

The power of the messianic case is not any single prophecy. It is
the *simultaneous convergence* of multiple independent requirements
that must all be true of the same person. [web:800][web:803]

Each condition narrows the field:

| Prophecy | Reference | Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Born in Bethlehem | Micah 5:2 | Specific village, not Jerusalem |
| From David's line | 2 Samuel 7:12-13 | Verifiable genealogy required |
| Appears before 70 AD | Daniel 9:26 | Hard chronological deadline |
| Cut off / killed | Daniel 9:26 | Death is part of the mission |
| Betrayed for 30 silver pieces | Zechariah 11:12 | Specific amount |
| Enters Jerusalem on a donkey | Zechariah 9:9 | Specific action |
| Hands and feet pierced | Psalm 22:16 | Written 1,000 years before crucifixion existed |
| Lots cast for his clothing | Psalm 22:18 | Specific detail, hostile parties |
| No bones broken | Psalm 34:20 | Specific physical detail |
| Buried in a rich man's tomb | Isaiah 53:9 | After criminal execution |
| Rises from the dead | Psalm 16:10 | "You will not let your Holy One see decay" |
| Gentiles stream to his name | Isaiah 49:6 | Global reach |

**The logical point:**
Any single prophecy could be coincidence. Any two could be coincidence.
But each additional fulfilled condition multiplies the improbability
exponentially. Professor Peter Stoner calculated the probability of
one person accidentally fulfilling just **eight** of these at
**1 in 10¹⁷** — a number that exceeds the number of atoms visible
to the naked eye. [web:800]

We are not dealing with eight. We are dealing with dozens — fulfilled
across independent parties (Roman soldiers, Jewish priests, Judas)
who had no motive or awareness of the prophecies they were fulfilling.

## The Davidic Lineage Problem — The Permanently Closed Door

The Messiah must be a **verifiable** descendant of David. [web:801]

Before 70 AD:
- Genealogical records were maintained in the Temple archives
- Tribal identity was verifiable and legally enforced
- The New Testament records Jesus's lineage — and no Jewish opponent
  in the first century ever challenged it. They challenged his
  authority, his interpretation of Torah, his claims — never his
  genealogy. Because it was checkable and correct.

After 70 AD:
- The Temple and its archives were destroyed by Rome
- All official genealogical records burned
- No mechanism remained to verify Davidic descent

**The implication is devastating:**
If Jesus is not the Messiah — *no one can ever be*, because the
verification mechanism no longer exists. The door did not just close.
It was destroyed. Any future claimant cannot prove what the prophecy
requires to be proven. [web:801]

This means one of three things is true:
1. Jesus is the Messiah — identified while verification was still possible
2. The Messiah came but we missed him — and we have no record of who
3. The prophecies permanently failed in 70 AD

Option 3 is the honest atheist position. Option 2 is incoherent —
a Messiah no one recorded. Option 1 is what the evidence points to.

## The Alternative Candidate Problem

The messianic case rests partly on **elimination**. If another
candidate exists who fits the sequence — the argument weakens.
No such candidate has ever been produced.

Here is the formal challenge:

> **Name one pre-70 AD figure who:**
> - Was born in Bethlehem
> - Had verified Davidic lineage
> - Was killed after appearing publicly as an anointed leader
> - Had the Temple destroyed *after* his death
> - Had followers who multiplied rather than scattered
> - Had an empty tomb that hostile contemporaries could not explain
> - Was identified by a hostile Jewish historian (Josephus)
> - Had a brother who did not believe during his lifetime convert
>   after his death
>
> **One name. Not Jesus. Go.**

In 2,000 years of Jewish scholarship — with every motivation to find
an alternative — no name has been produced. The silence is not a gap
in the argument. It is the argument. [web:807]

## The Two Portraits Problem — Your Own Talmud Admits It

The rabbis themselves recognized an irreconcilable tension in the
prophetic texts long before Christianity:

- **Daniel 7:13** — the Son of Man comes *on the clouds of heaven*
  (glorious, triumphant, divine)
- **Zechariah 9:9** — the king comes *humble, on a donkey*
  (lowly, suffering)

These cannot be the same event. The Talmud in **Sanhedrin 98a**
records Rabbi Alexandri noting this contradiction directly — and
the rabbinic resolution was: *it depends on Israel's merit.*

The Christian resolution — two comings — solves the same problem
the Talmud acknowledged, using the same texts, **before 70 AD.**

We did not invent the two-coming framework to rescue a failed
candidate. We inherited a tension your own tradition documented —
and resolved it before the window closed.

## Isaiah 53 — Your Own Ancient Sources Read It as Messianic

The standard Jewish counter is: "Isaiah 53 refers to Israel
collectively, not a Messiah."

But this reading is **not** the ancient Jewish reading:

- **Targum Jonathan** (ancient Jewish Aramaic translation) opens
  Isaiah 53 with: *"Behold my servant the Messiah shall prosper"*
- **Sanhedrin 98b** (Talmud) identifies the Messiah's name as
  "the leper scholar" — drawn directly from Isaiah 53:4
  ("he bore our sicknesses")
- **Rabbi Moshe Alshich** (16th century): "Our rabbis with one voice
  accept and affirm the opinion that the prophet is speaking of
  the King Messiah"

The collective Israel reading became dominant specifically as a
**counter-missionary move** after Christianity spread. The ancient
sources are against it. [web:800]

**The devastating point from the text itself:**
Isaiah 53:9 — "He was assigned a grave with the wicked, and with
the rich in his death."

How does a collective nation end up buried with the rich after being
assigned a grave with the wicked? The verse describes an individual
executed as a criminal but buried in a rich man's tomb. That is not
a nation. That is one person. It is Jesus — buried in Joseph of
Arimathea's tomb.

## Zechariah 12:10 — God Pierced

This is the verse the Jewish side almost never addresses:

> "They will look on **me** whom they have **pierced** — and they
> will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child."

Notice the pronoun shift: **me** (first person — God speaking)
→ **him** (third person — the one who was pierced).

God is speaking in the first person about being *pierced*. Then
refers to the pierced one in the third person as an individual
who will be mourned. This is either:
- A description of God himself being pierced — in human form
- A grammatical error in scripture

The Trinitarian/incarnational reading resolves it coherently.
No other framework does.

## Psalm 110:1 — Two Divine Persons

> "The LORD (*YHWH*) said to my Lord (*Adonai*): 'Sit at my right hand
> until I make your enemies a footstool.'"

David is writing. David calls someone "my Lord." That someone is
distinct from YHWH — yet sits at YHWH's right hand.

Jesus quotes this Psalm at his trial (Mark 12:35-37) and asks:
*"If David calls him Lord — how is he his son?"*

The crowd could not answer. The question exposes that the Messiah
must be *both* David's descendant (human) *and* David's Lord
(divine). Two natures. One person. The Trinitarian answer.
