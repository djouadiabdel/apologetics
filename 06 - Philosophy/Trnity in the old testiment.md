Argument 1 — The Linguistic Argument: Echad Is Not Absolute Unity

The claim: Deuteronomy 6:4 — "Hear O Israel, Yahweh our God, Yahweh is one" — proves God is numerically singular, ruling out any internal plurality.

The linguistic reality: The Hebrew word translated "one" is echad — and echad does not mean absolute, indivisible singularity. It means unified oneness — a composite unity.

The evidence is in the same Torah:

    Genesis 2:24 — "A man shall leave his father and mother and cleave to his wife and they shall become one [echad] flesh."

Two distinct persons becoming one. The same word. The same author. The same covenant document.

If echad in Genesis 2:24 describes a unity of two distinct persons without contradiction, then echad in Deuteronomy 6:4 cannot be used to rule out a unity of distinct persons within God.

The Hebrew word for absolute, indivisible singularity is yachid — used in Genesis 22:2 for Isaac as Abraham's "only" son. The Shema does not use yachid. It uses echad. This is not accidental — it is the precise word for unified plurality.

Logical form: The objection commits the fallacy of equivocation — assuming echad means absolute singularity when the Hebrew text itself demonstrates it means composite unity.


Argument 2 — The Textual Argument: Isaiah 48:16

The text: Isaiah 48:12-16 — Yahweh is unmistakably the speaker. He identifies himself:

"I am the first and I am the last." (v.12)

Then in verse 16:

"And now the Lord God has sent me and his Spirit."

The argument: The speaker throughout is Yahweh. But in verse 16, this same speaker says that Yahweh has sent him and his Spirit.

This is not a human prophet speaking — the surrounding context is Yahweh himself declaring his divine identity, his creative power, his calling of Israel. And yet this divine speaker distinguishes himself from the Yahweh who sends him, and from the Spirit who accompanies him.

Three distinguishable presences within a single divine identity:

    Yahweh who is sent

    Yahweh who sends

    The Spirit of Yahweh

This does not say "Trinity" in Nicene vocabulary. But it directly contradicts the claim that the Old Testament presents God as absolutely undifferentiated. A strict numerical monotheism — one person, one subject, no internal distinctions — cannot coherently account for this verse without special pleading.

Logical form: The objection must either explain this verse or concede that the Old Testament itself presents divine plurality. It cannot use the Old Testament to disprove Trinitarian plurality while ignoring the Old Testament's own plurality passages.


Argument 3 — The Theological Argument: Progressive Revelation Is Not Contradiction

The objection: If the Trinity were true, why doesn't the Old Testament say so explicitly?

The response: This objection proves too much. By the same logic:

    Why doesn't the Old Testament explicitly mention the resurrection of the dead in the same terms as Paul in 1 Corinthians 15?

    Why doesn't Genesis explicitly define the Mosaic covenant before Moses?

    Why did God tell Moses in Exodus 6:3 that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob knew him as El Shaddai but not as Yahweh — a deeper revelation of the same God?

Both Christianity and Islam accept progressive revelation — God disclosing more of himself and his purposes across time through successive covenants. Muslims accept this explicitly in their doctrine of abrogation and in their acknowledgment that the Torah and Gospel preceded the Quran as earlier revelations.

The pattern of the Old Testament is precisely this: each covenant comes with deeper knowledge of who God is. Abraham received more than Noah. Moses received more than Abraham. Jeremiah 31 explicitly prophesies a new covenant coming — one different from and deeper than the Mosaic covenant.

"Behold, the days are coming, declares the Lord, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel." (Jeremiah 31:31)

If this new covenant brings deeper revelation — and the Old Testament pattern demands it does — then the fullest disclosure of God's inner nature arriving with the new covenant's inaugurator (Jesus) and the outpouring of the Spirit is exactly what the Old Testament structure predicts.

The absence of explicit Trinitarian vocabulary in the Old Testament is not an argument against the Trinity. It is precisely what progressive revelation predicts.

Logical form: The objection commits the argument from silence fallacy — assuming that the absence of explicit Nicene vocabulary disproves a doctrine whose full articulation was to come with a later covenant the Old Testament itself anticipates.


Argument 4 — The Grammatical Argument: Elohim

The standard Hebrew word for God throughout the Old Testament is Elohim — a grammatically plural form. The singular would be Eloah.

Elohim takes singular verbs when referring to the God of Israel — it is not polytheism. But the deliberate use of a plural morphology for the singular God of Israel is at minimum consistent with a God whose unity is composite rather than absolute. The rabbinical explanation that this is a "plural of majesty" is possible — but it is an interpretation imposed on the text, not demanded by it. The Trinitarian reading — that the plural form hints at the plural-in-unity nature of God — is at least equally grammatically valid.

Summary of the Logical Case
Objection	Fallacy	Response
Shema proves absolute singularity	Equivocation on echad	Echad means composite unity in the same texts
OT never mentions Trinity	Argument from silence	Progressive revelation predicts later fuller disclosure
Isaiah 48:16 is just a prophet speaking	Special pleading	The speaker is Yahweh throughout — context is explicit
No Trinity = no Christianity	False dichotomy	The OT lays the foundation; the NT completes the disclosure
The Conclusion

The Old Testament does not explicitly teach the Nicene Trinity in its full formulation — no Christian scholar claims it does. What it does is this:

    Present God's unity in language (echad) that is composite rather than absolute

    Contain texts (Isaiah 48:16) that present distinguishable persons within the divine identity

    Operate through a pattern of progressive revelation that explicitly anticipates a new covenant bringing deeper knowledge of God

    Use grammatical forms (Elohim) consistent with a plural-in-unity divine nature

The argument that the Old Testament disproves the Trinity is not just weak — it fails on its own linguistic terms. The Shema, the go-to refutation, uses the precise Hebrew word that in every other context in the Torah means unified plurality.
