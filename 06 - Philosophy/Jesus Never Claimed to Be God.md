Jesus Never Claimed to Be God
The Objection

Jesus never said "I am God, worship me." Therefore the divinity of Christ is a later invention imposed on a Jewish prophet who taught submission to God.
The Response

The I AM Statements — John 8:58

"Before Abraham was, I AM."

The crowd immediately picked up stones to kill him — the Jewish penalty for blasphemy. Why? Because I AM (ego eimi) is the divine name from Exodus 3:14 — what God said to Moses at the burning bush. Jesus did not say "I was" — he used the present tense eternal form. The Jewish reaction proves they understood exactly what he was claiming.

John 10:30

"I and the Father are one."

Again, the crowd picked up stones. When Jesus asked why, they said: "Because you, a mere man, claim to be God." (John 10:33). His opponents understood his claim better than modern objectors.

Mark 14:62

At his trial, the high priest asked directly: "Are you the Messiah, the Son of the Blessed One?"

Jesus answered: "I am. And you will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of the Mighty One and coming on the clouds of heaven."

This is a direct claim combining two messianic passages — Daniel 7:13 and Psalm 110:1 — that the high priest immediately recognized as a claim to divine status. His response: "Blasphemy."

The divine titles Jesus accepted:

    Thomas called him "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28) — Jesus accepted it without correction

    He accepted worship (proskuneo) — Matthew 28:17 — the same word used for worshipping God

    He claimed authority to forgive sins — which the scribes correctly noted "only God can do" (Mark 2:7)

The Psalm 110 argument — Jesus's own logic:

Matthew 22:41-46 — Jesus asks the Pharisees: "How can the Messiah be David's son if David himself calls him Lord?"

"The Lord said to my Lord: sit at my right hand."

Jesus is pointing out that David's own son — his descendant — is also David's Lord. This only makes sense if the Messiah is more than a human descendant. Jesus is using Israel's own scripture to establish his own transcendent identity. Nobody answered him.
The Proverbs 30 Argument — "What Is His Son's Name?"

Proverbs 30:4

"Who has ascended into heaven and come down? Who has gathered the wind in his fists? Who has wrapped up the waters in a garment? Who has established all the ends of the earth? What is his name, and what is his son's name? Surely you know!"

This is written by Agur — in the Hebrew wisdom tradition, centuries before Christ. He asks: who is God? And then immediately asks: what is his son's name?

This is not an accident. The structure of the question assumes the son exists and has a name — it is asking the reader to identify both. The irony is devastating: a pre-Christian Hebrew sage is already asking about God's son, and the answer the New Testament gives is Jesus.

No monotheist who denies the Son can satisfactorily answer Agur's question. It hangs in the text waiting for an answer that tawhid structurally cannot provide.
