This is the sharpest version of the problem. Here is exactly what it looks like laid out properly.

## **The Central Trap Ibn Taymiyya Walks Into**

His entire strategy in *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* rests on this claim:

> *"The Torah, Psalms, and Gospel — in their original transmitted form — do not actually support the Trinity, Incarnation, or divine Sonship. Christians misread their own books."* [abdurrahman](https://abdurrahman.org/2014/12/12/the-correct-response-for-those-who-altered-the-religion-of-jesus/)

He has to say this because he already conceded the text is not corrupted. So the books are intact — but he claims they do not say what Christians think they say.

This is the position. And it collapses completely when you open the Old Testament.

## **Proverbs 30:4 — The Verse He Cannot Handle**

The text reads:

> *"Who has ascended into heaven and descended? Who has gathered the wind in His fists? Who has wrapped the waters in His garment? Who has established all the ends of the earth? What is His name — and **what is the name of His Son** — if you know?"*

This is **pre-Christian Hebrew scripture.** It was written around 700-900 BCE. [gotquestions](https://www.gotquestions.org/what-is-the-name-of-His-son.html)

Notice what is happening:

- The question is about **God** — who established the ends of the earth, who controls wind and water
- And in the **same breath** — it asks for the name of **His Son**
- The Son is presented not as a human king metaphorically — but as someone connected to the same cosmic acts attributed to God Himself [gotquestions](https://www.gotquestions.org/what-is-the-name-of-His-son.html)

The Jewish Talmud itself interprets Psalm 2:7 — *"You are My Son, today I have begotten you"* — as referring to **the Messiah specifically**, not just any king. The Babylonian Talmud in Sukkah 52a quotes this Psalm as: *"The Holy One, Blessed be He, says to Messiah ben David: Ask of Me anything..."* [reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/DebateReligion/comments/1alnm4i/proverbs_304_is_not_referring_to_jesuspbuh/)

So the Messiah being called **God's Son** in a unique sense — this is not a Christian invention imposed onto the text. It is the **Jewish reading of their own scripture.**

### **Ibn Taymiyya's Response and Why It Fails**

He argues in *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* Chapter 3 that these passages were being claimed by Christians as proof of the hypostases but represent only metaphorical language about kings and prophets. [abdurrahman](https://abdurrahman.org/2014/12/12/the-correct-response-for-those-who-altered-the-religion-of-jesus/)

The problem is threefold:

**First** — Proverbs 30:4 is not about a king. It is about the act of creation itself. The "Son" is connected to cosmological acts — gathering wind, wrapping waters, establishing the earth. No Israelite king did these things. The text demands a reading that goes beyond human royalty.

**Second** — Ibn Taymiyya conceded the text is intact. So if the intact text asks *"what is the name of His Son?"* in the context of divine creation — his only move is to say it means something other than what it plainly says. That is precisely the **tahrif al-ma'na** he accuses Christians of — he is reinterpreting the text to avoid its plain implication.

**Third** — He never engages the **cumulative weight** of these passages together. He responds to each one in isolation and never confronts the pattern.

## **Isaiah 9:6 — The Name He Cannot Explain Away**

> *"For to us a child is born, to us a son is given... and His name will be called: **Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace.**"*

Ibn Taymiyya has to deal with this verse in *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* because it is perhaps the strongest single Trinitarian passage in the entire Old Testament. [muslimhope](https://www.muslimhope.com/RebuttalToAlJawabAsSahih3_UniversalityOfMohammedsProphethood.html)

His response: the titles are honorific, the way ancient Near Eastern kings were given divine-sounding throne names.

### **Why This Fails Completely**

**Problem 1 — El Gibbor — Mighty God**

The Hebrew title *El Gibbor* — Mighty God — is used in Isaiah 10:21 to refer to **YHWH Himself.** Not a metaphor. Not a throne name. The exact same construction used for God directly is applied to this coming child.

For Ibn Taymiyya's reading to work — Isaiah would have to be giving a human child the exact divine name he uses elsewhere exclusively for YHWH. That is not honorific language. That is identity language.

**Problem 2 — Avi Ad — Everlasting Father**

No human king in Israelite history is called *Avi Ad* — Father of Eternity. This is not a known royal title in ancient Near Eastern coronation literature. Ibn Taymiyya's appeal to court language fails here because the language has no parallel in any known human coronation context.

**Problem 3 — He Ignored the Context**

Isaiah 9:6 comes immediately after Isaiah 7:14 — *"A virgin will conceive and bear a son, and His name will be called Immanuel — God with us."*

Two chapters. Two supernatural birth announcements. Two divine naming formulas. Ibn Taymiyya addresses them separately and never confronts what they mean **together** — that Isaiah is building a coherent portrait of a coming figure who is simultaneously human (born, given) and divine (El Gibbor, Immanuel).

This is not a coincidence of two verses. This is Isaiah's sustained theological argument. Ibn Taymiyya dismantles individual trees and ignores the forest.

***

## **Psalm 110:1 — The Verse Jesus Used and Ibn Taymiyya Avoided**

> *"YHWH said to my Adon — sit at My right hand until I make your enemies your footstool."*

This is the most quoted Old Testament verse in the entire New Testament — cited over 20 times. [scielo.org](http://www.scielo.org.za/scielo.php?script=sci_arttext&pid=S2305-08532015000100034)

The structure is:
- YHWH (God) speaks to **someone called David's Lord** (Adon)
- David calls this person his own Lord — meaning this person is **above David**
- Yet this person is described as David's descendant elsewhere (Psalm 2)

So: descended from David, yet David calls him Lord. Seated at the right hand of YHWH — a position of equal authority.

Jesus himself uses this verse in Matthew 22:41-46 to pose the exact question: *"If the Messiah is David's son — why does David call him Lord?"* The Pharisees could not answer. [jurnal.widyaagape.ac](https://jurnal.widyaagape.ac.id/index.php/jrsc/article/download/232/154)

### **Ibn Taymiyya's Non-Response**

In *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* he argues the "sitting at the right hand" is not literal divine co-equality but an expression of honor — like a king honoring a minister. [muslimhope](https://www.muslimhope.com/RebuttalToAlJawabAsSahih3_UniversalityOfMohammedsProphethood.html)

This fails because:

**First** — the specific act of sitting at YHWH's own right hand has no parallel in Israelite royal or prophetic literature for any human figure. It is not a known idiom for ministerial honor.

**Second** — the problem Jesus posed remains completely unanswered: **how is the Messiah both David's descendant and David's Lord?** Ibn Taymiyya never engages this question directly. He dismantles the "right hand" language but leaves the **logical problem of the verse** completely untouched.

**Third** — Verse 4 of the same Psalm: *"You are a priest forever according to the order of Melchizedek."* No Davidic king served as priest. The two offices — king and priest — were constitutionally separate in Israel. The only person in Israelite history who combined both was Melchizedek — a mysterious figure who appears once in Genesis with no genealogy and no death recorded. The Psalm is describing someone with a **non-human priestly order.** Ibn Taymiyya says nothing meaningful about this.

## **The Core Problem With His Entire Methodology**

Here is what makes *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* ultimately an intellectual failure despite its size and learning:

Ibn Taymiyya's method is: take each verse, propose an alternative reading, declare the Christian reading refuted. Repeat for 800 pages.

What he never does:

### **1. He Never Accounts for the Pattern**

Proverbs 30:4, Isaiah 7:14, Isaiah 9:6, Isaiah 53, Psalm 2, Psalm 22, Psalm 110, Micah 5:2 (*"whose origins are from of old, from eternity"*) — these are not isolated verses. They form a **coherent cumulative portrait** of a coming figure who is simultaneously human and divine, born and eternal, servant and Lord.

Ibn Taymiyya never asks: why does the Old Testament, written across 1,000 years by dozens of authors, consistently produce this portrait? He addresses each tree and never looks at the forest.

### **2. He Never Engages the Early Fathers**

Christian Trinitarian theology was not invented at Nicaea in 325 CE. Justin Martyr (150 CE), Ignatius of Antioch (107 CE), and Clement of Rome (96 CE) all wrote Trinitarian language within living memory of the apostles. [muslimhope](https://www.muslimhope.com/RebuttalToAlJawabAsSahih3_UniversalityOfMohammedsProphethood.html)

Ibn Taymiyya claims the Trinity was a later corruption. But the earliest non-canonical Christian writings — predating most New Testament manuscripts he would accept — already contain it. He never engages this evidence seriously.

### **3. He Ignores the Inescapable Internal Logic**

If the text is intact — and he said it is — and the intact text contains:

- A divine Son in Proverbs 30:4
- A child called Mighty God in Isaiah 9:6
- A Messiah who is David's Lord in Psalm 110
- A servant who bears the sins of many in Isaiah 53
- A ruler whose origins are from eternity in Micah 5:2

Then his only option is to say: all of these were misread for 2,000 years by Jews and Christians alike, and the correct reading — which happens to align with Islam — was preserved by Muhammad in 7th century Arabia with no access to the original Hebrew texts.

That is not a scholarly argument. That is a conclusion in search of a methodology.

***

## **The One-Line Verdict on Al-Jawab al-Sahih**

Ibn Taymiyya conceded the text is intact, then spent 800 pages arguing the intact text means something other than what it plainly says — without ever confronting the cumulative pattern, the early patristic evidence, or the internal logic of the question Jesus himself posed from Psalm 110 that nobody has ever answered.

It is the longest ad hoc argument in Islamic history.
