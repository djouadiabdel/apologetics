## The Test Jesus Established

**Matthew 7:15-20:**
> "Watch out for false prophets. They come to you in
> sheep's clothing, but inwardly they are ferocious
> wolves. By their fruit you will recognize them."

**Matthew 12:33-37:**
> "A tree is recognized by its fruit... For out of the
> overflow of the heart the mouth speaks. A good man
> brings good things out of the good stored up in him...
> But I tell you that everyone will have to give account
> on the day of judgment for every empty word they have
> spoken."

Jesus did not give a complicated test. He gave two things:
1. Watch what a prophet **produces** — the pattern of
   their life and teaching
2. Watch what a prophet **says** — words reveal the
   heart, and words spoken in God's name carry weight

This is the standard. Now apply it.

## The Evidence — Muhammad's Curse Hadiths

**Sahih Muslim 2601:**
> "O Allah, I am a human being and for any person amongst
> Muslims upon whom I hurl malediction or invoke curse
> or give him whipping, make it a source of purity
> and mercy."

Similar reports appear in **Sahih al-Bukhari** with
consistent structure. The pattern is:

Step 1: Muhammad invokes a curse or malediction  
on a specific person  
Step 2: A built-in corrective clause follows —  
if this was misapplied, convert it to mercy


This is not a single incident. It is a **reported
practice** — documented across multiple hadith
collections, treated as normative enough to record
and preserve.

---

## Why the Pattern Is Significant

### Invoking a Divine Curse Is Not a Casual Act

Calling down God's curse on a named individual
is a high-stakes theological claim. It assumes:
- That person deserves divine judgment
- That you have the moral and spiritual clarity
  to identify them as deserving it
- That God's authority stands behind the invocation

You do not casually call God's curse on someone.
The act signals **strong moral confidence** that
you are aligned with divine justice.

### The Disclaimer Contradicts the Confidence

The corrective clause — "if I was wrong, convert
it to mercy" — acknowledges the **possibility of
misapplication.** That pairing is a direct
contradiction:

- The invocation says: I am confident enough to
  call God's judgment on this person
- The disclaimer says: But I may have been wrong

These two cannot coexist under a model of precise
divine guidance. A prophet who genuinely speaks
under divine revelation when pronouncing judgment
does not need a theological safety net in case
the judgment misfired.

## The Bayesian Frame — Two Hypotheses

### Hypothesis 1 — Muhammad Has Reliable Divine Guidance

If Muhammad is operating under ongoing, precise
divine guidance when invoking God's authority:

**What we would expect:**
- Strong alignment between judgment invoked and
  justice deserved — divine guidance reduces error
- No need for a corrective disclaimer — the prophet
  would not misinvoke God's curse
- Precise speech when pronouncing God's judgment
  on named individuals

**What we actually see:**
- A recurring disclaimer acknowledging possible
  misapplication
- A theological safety net built into the practice

This evidence is **unexpected under H1.** It does
not fit the model of uniquely precise prophetic
guidance.

### Hypothesis 2 — Muhammad Is a Human Leader with Normal Limitations

If Muhammad is a human religious leader operating
under normal human psychology:

**What we would expect:**
- Emotional reactions — cursing perceived enemies
  in moments of anger or frustration
- A post-hoc theological safety net installed
  to cover cases where the curse may have been
  unjust
- The invocation-plus-disclaimer pattern as
  a natural human hedge

**What we actually see:**
- Exactly this pattern

This evidence is **highly expected under H2.**
It fits the model of a human cult leader managing
his own emotional reactions and protecting against
the reputational damage of unjust curses.

**Bayesian conclusion:** When evidence is more
expected under one hypothesis than another, it
increases the probability of that hypothesis.
The invocation-plus-disclaimer pattern raises
the probability of H2 and lowers the probability
of H1. Not a proof — but a meaningful shift in
the balance of evidence.

## The Islamic Framing Makes This Worse

This is not just a problem for a random religious
teacher. The Quran presents Muhammad as:

**Surah 33:21:**
> لَّقَدۡ كَانَ لَكُمۡ فِي رَسُولِ ٱللَّهِ أُسۡوَةٌ حَسَنَةٌ
>
> "Indeed in the Messenger of Allah you have an
> excellent example."

**Surah 21:107:**
> وَمَآ أَرۡسَلۡنَٰكَ إِلَّا رَحۡمَةٗ لِّلۡعَٰلَمِينَ
>
> "We have not sent you except as a mercy to the worlds."

**Surah 33:40:**
> خَاتَمَ ٱلنَّبِيِّـۧنَ
>
> "The seal of the prophets."

If Muhammad is:
- The final prophet — no correction is coming after him
- The excellent example — Muslims model their behavior
  on him
- A mercy to all worlds — his practice is universally
  normative

Then a prophet presented as the **final, exemplary,
universal model** of prophetic guidance has a **built-in
corrective clause for his own possible unjust curses.**

That is not what mercy to the worlds looks like.
That is not what the seal of the prophets looks like.
The Islamic framing of Muhammad elevates the problem —
it does not diminish it.

## The Contrast — Jesus Under the Same Test

Apply Matthew 7 and 12 to Jesus under the same
standard:

| Category | Muhammad | Jesus |
|---|---|---|
| **Insulted** | Cursed opponents with disclaimer | "Father, forgive them" — Luke 23:34 |
| **Betrayed** | Cursed enemies | Healed the servant's ear — Luke 22:51 |
| **Facing death** | No recorded pattern of restraint | Voluntary surrender — John 10:18 |
| **Teaching on enemies** | Warfare permitted against them | "Bless those who curse you" — Luke 6:28 |
| **Words invoking God** | Built-in disclaimer for misapplication | No disclaimer — speaks with authority |
| **Pattern of speech** | Curses acknowledged as possibly unjust | "Not as the scribes — as one having authority" |

**Luke 6:28** — Jesus teaches:
> "Bless those who curse you, pray for those who
> mistreat you."

The teacher is consistent with the teaching.
The practice matches the standard set. No
theological safety net. No corrective clause
installed for unjust curses. The trajectory
when provoked is restraint and forgiveness —
without exception in the Gospel record.

## The Precision Point — Imprecatory Language Is Not the Issue

This argument is **not** claiming that:
- Prophets can never speak harshly
- Imprecatory psalms and prophetic curses are wrong
- Calling for God's justice against evil is invalid

The Hebrew Bible has strong imprecatory language.
Psalm 109. The prophets calling judgment on nations.
Elijah calling down fire. None of that is the debate.

**The debate is precision when speaking in God's name.**

There is a categorical difference between:
- A Psalm lamenting injustice and calling for God's
  judgment on enemies — honest emotional expression
  in prayer
- A prophet invoking God's curse on a specific named
  person WITH a built-in disclaimer in case it was
  wrong

The second form requires claiming the authority to
call divine judgment — while simultaneously admitting
you may have been wrong to do so. That pairing is
incoherent under a model of precise revelation.
