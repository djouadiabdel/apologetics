## The Test — From the Torah Itself

**Deuteronomy 18:20-22:**
> וְאַךְ הַנָּבִיא אֲשֶׁר יָזִיד לְדַבֵּר דָּבָר בִּשְׁמִי
> אֵת אֲשֶׁר לֹא-צִוִּיתִיו לְדַבֵּר וַאֲשֶׁר יְדַבֵּר
> בְּשֵׁם אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים וּמֵת הַנָּבִיא הַהוּא
>
> "But a prophet who presumes to speak in my name
> anything I have not commanded, or a prophet who
> speaks in the name of other gods, is to be put
> to death.
>
> You may say to yourselves, 'How can we know when
> a message has not been spoken by the LORD?'
>
> **If what a prophet proclaims in the name of the
> LORD does not take place or come true, that is
> a message the LORD has not spoken. That prophet
> has spoken presumptuously. Do not be alarmed."**

The Torah gives a binary, empirical test:

IF the prophet's words spoken in God's name  
come true → credible  
IF they do not come true → false prophet,  
do not fear him


This is not a vague spiritual standard. It is a
**falsifiability test written into the Law of Moses.**
A prophet is verified or disqualified by the
accuracy of their specific claims made in God's name.

## Why This Test Applies to Muhammad

Muhammad explicitly claimed this lineage. The Quran
presents him as the successor of Moses:

**Surah 46:12:**
> وَمِن قَبۡلِهِۦ كِتَٰبُ مُوسَىٰٓ إِمَامٗا وَرَحۡمَةٗ
>
> "Before it was the scripture of Moses — a guide
> and a mercy."

**Surah 61:6:**
> وَإِذۡ قَالَ عِيسَى ٱبۡنُ مَرۡيَمَ يَٰبَنِيٓ إِسۡرَٰٓءِيلَ
> إِنِّي رَسُولُ ٱللَّهِ إِلَيۡكُم مُّصَدِّقٗا لِّمَا
> بَيۡنَ يَدَيَّ مِنَ ٱلتَّوۡرَىٰةِ
>
> "And when Jesus said: O Children of Israel,
> I am the messenger of Allah to you, confirming
> what came before me of the Torah."

Muhammad's prophethood is presented as standing
in the same prophetic line that the Torah governs.
The Quran explicitly confirms the Torah as
authoritative — including Deuteronomy 18. Therefore
the test of Deuteronomy 18 **applies by the Quran's
own logic.** Either the Torah's prophet test is
valid — and Muhammad must pass it — or the Torah
is not authoritative — and the Quran's confirmation
of it collapses.

## Test 1 — The Siege of Constantinople

**The Hadith — Sahih Bukhari 2924, Sunan Abu Dawud:**
> "Verily you will conquer Constantinople. What a
> wonderful leader will her leader be, and what a
> wonderful army will that army be."

This was spoken as a prophetic statement — not a
wish, not a prayer, but a declaration in prophetic
framing about a specific named city being conquered.

**Historical record:**
- Constantinople was **not conquered** in Muhammad's
  lifetime
- It was **not conquered** for 800 years after Muhammad
- It was finally taken in **1453 AD** by Ottoman Sultan
  Mehmed II

**The problem:**
Deuteronomy 18 does not say "the prophecy will
eventually come true in a millennium." It says
"if it does not take place." Eight hundred years
of non-fulfillment is not a delayed prophecy.
It is a failed prophecy that happened to
coincidentally come true later via historical
expansion that was already in progress.

**The Muslim response:**
"It was fulfilled eventually — Mehmed II."

**The counter:**
If prophecies can be fulfilled 800 years later
by entirely different people in entirely different
political contexts — the test has no teeth. You
can say anything will happen eventually and wait
for coincidence to confirm it. Deuteronomy 18
requires specific, timely fulfillment — not
millennium-scale coincidence.

## Test 3 — The Direct Failed Prediction

**Sahih Bukhari 3629:**
> "The Hour will not be established till a fire
> comes out of the land of Hijaz, illuminating
> the necks of the camels at Busra."

This is a specific geographical prophetic sign —
a fire from Hijaz visible to camels at Busra
(in modern Syria). No such fire has occurred.
Muhammad's companions were told this was an
imminent sign of the Last Hour. It has not
happened in 1,400 years.

## Test 4 — The Timing Problem in Surah 30

**Surah 30:2-4:**
> غُلِبَتِ ٱلرُّومُ ۝ فِيٓ أَدۡنَى ٱلۡأَرۡضِ وَهُم مِّنۢ
> بَعۡدِ غَلَبِهِمۡ سَيَغۡلِبُونَ ۝ فِي بِضۡعِ سِنِينَ
>
> "The Romans have been defeated in the nearest
> land. But after their defeat they will overcome
> **within a few years** (*fi bid'i sinin*)."

*Bid'i sinin* in Arabic means 3-9 years — "a few
years" is the universally accepted range.

**Historical record:**
The Byzantine-Sasanian war ended with Byzantine
victory in **628 AD** — approximately 9 years
after the defeat referenced. Muslims cite this
as fulfillment at the very outer edge of "a few."

**The problem:**
This is less a supernatural prophecy and more
an observation of an ongoing war and a reasonable
prediction that the losing side would recover.
The Roman-Persian conflict was cyclical and
well-known. Predicting Byzantine recovery was
not a high-risk prophetic claim — it was a
reasonable geopolitical observation. Deuteronomy
18 requires precision that exceeds educated
guessing.

## The Sharpest Application — The Test He Cannot Escape

The most airtight application of Deuteronomy 18
to Muhammad is not about specific predictions.
It is structural:

**Deuteronomy 18:20:**
> "A prophet who speaks in the name of other gods
> is to be put to death."

The question is not just whether predictions came
true. It is whether the prophet is speaking for
the God of Israel — YHWH — or for another deity.

**The argument:**

1. The God of the Torah has a name — YHWH (יהוה)
2. YHWH revealed himself to Moses, the Patriarchs,
   and Israel — with a specific covenantal identity
3. The Quran's Allah does not identify as YHWH,
   does not affirm the Sinai covenant, explicitly
   denies the sonship of Jesus and the Trinity —
   positions YHWH affirmed progressively through
   his own prophets
4. Deuteronomy 18:20 says a prophet who speaks
   in the name of **other gods** is a false prophet

**The Muslim counter:**
"Allah IS the God of Abraham, Moses, and Jesus —
same God, different name."

**The counter to the counter:**
If it is the same God — then that God:
- Made an eternal covenant with Israel through
  the Davidic line (2 Samuel 7)
- Promised the Messiah from David's line would
  rule forever
- Described himself through Isaiah as having
  a Son (Isaiah 9:6) whose origins are eternal
  (Micah 5:2)
- Described his servant bearing sin for others
  (Isaiah 53)

If Allah is the same God as YHWH — he cannot
then deny everything YHWH said about himself
through his own prophets. Either YHWH's prophets
were accurate — and the Torah test stands — or
they were corrupted — and Muhammad cannot claim
to stand in YHWH's prophetic line.

Every exit closes another door.

## The Internal Islamic Problem

The Quran explicitly tells Muhammad to use the
Torah's framework for verification:

**Surah 10:94:**
> فَإِن كُنتَ فِي شَكٍّ مِّمَّآ أَنزَلۡنَآ إِلَيۡكَ
> فَسۡـَٔلِ ٱلَّذِينَ يَقۡرَءُونَ ٱلۡكِتَٰبَ مِن قَبۡلِكَ
>
> "If you are in doubt about what We have revealed
> to you — ask those who have been reading the
> Book before you."

The Book they were reading included Deuteronomy 18.
Allah directed Muhammad to verify his prophethood
using the very scripture that contains the false
prophet test. If the Torah was the verification
standard — the test in that Torah applies.
Muhammad cannot simultaneously:
- Appeal to Torah authority for verification
- Exempt himself from the Torah's own test

## Complete Syllogism



P1. Deuteronomy 18 gives an empirical test for  
prophets — specific predictions must come true  
P2. The Quran confirms the Torah as authoritative  
(Surah 3:3, 2:41) and directs Muhammad to be  
verified by Torah readers (Surah 10:94)  
P3. Muhammad made specific predictions in God's name  
that did not materialize as stated  
P4. Muhammad predicted events tied to timelines  
that did not match fulfillment  
P5. Under Deuteronomy 18:20, a prophet who speaks  
for another God is also disqualified  
P6. The God Muhammad describes contradicts YHWH's  
own self-revelation through his prophets  
C1. By the Torah standard the Quran endorses —  
Muhammad fails the empirical test  
C2. By the same Torah standard — speaking for a  
God who contradicts YHWH's prophets — Muhammad  
fails the identity test  
C3. The Muslim cannot reject Deuteronomy 18 without  
undermining the Quran's own endorsement of  
Torah authority
